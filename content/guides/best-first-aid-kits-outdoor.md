## What to look for (buying checklist)

### 1) Size & activity match
- Day hike: minimal kit + blister care + basic wound care
- Backpacking: more bandaging + pain relief + anti‑diarrheal
- Family/car camping: bigger kit with duplicates and more tools

### 2) Realistic contents
The best kits have usable quantities of the boring stuff:
- bandages, gauze, tape
- antiseptic wipes
- blister care

### 3) Organization
Look for labeled compartments and a kit you can repack quickly.

### 4) Refill and maintenance
A kit is a living thing: refill after trips and audit a few times per year.

### 5) Training matters
Gear doesn’t replace skills. Basic first aid knowledge multiplies the value of any kit.

## A practical outdoor kit setup (simple)
1) Primary kit sized to your activity
2) Tiny mini kit you always carry (blisters + bandages)
3) Refill plan (restock after each trip)

## Common mistakes
1) Buying a kit that’s too small
2) No blister plan
3) Never opening the kit

## FAQ

### Best kit size for hiking?
Small enough to carry, big enough to handle cuts and blisters.

### Build your own?
Custom kits can be great. Many people start prebuilt and customize.

### Do I need a trauma kit?
Not as a primary kit for most hikes. As an add‑on pouch, it can make sense.

### Replace items how often?
Check after trips and audit a few times per year.
